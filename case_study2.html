Product Design Case Study

Self-Ordering Kiosk for Restaurants

Designing a seamless touchscreen interface that mitigates long queue times and order errors through user-centered visual guidance and meal customization.

Role UI/UX designer
Timeline 4 Weeks
Platform Touchscreen Kiosk
Deliverables Figma Prototype, Wireframes and Testing Reports

01 Problem

Queues waste up to 50% of limited dining breaks, while manual counter ordering in noisy halls creates a 64% error rate.

In high-traffic cafeteria and quick-service dining settings, traditional manual counter ordering creates substantial bottlenecks during rush hours. Verbal communication in noisy environments often results in inaccurate customizations, worker fatigue, and slow throughput.

02 Research

Field observations and 50+ user survey responses revealed two distinct user groups with contrasting tech-literacy.
Kavindu Perera (Student)
High Tech Confidence · Severe Time Constraints

"I just want to get my food quickly, configure it my way, and pay with QR."

  • Context: Needs to place orders during tight 15-minute lecture breaks.
  • Needs: Rapid category navigation, instant payment options, and clear checkout flows.
Shanthi Wijesinghe (Office Worker)
Low Tech Confidence · Prefers Clear Guidance

"I want an uncomplicated way to place my order without feeling rushed by queues."

  • Context: Seeks a relaxing, stress-free dining experience during short lunch hours.
  • Needs: Highly readable typography, large visual hit targets, and progressive disclosure.

1. Peak Queue Bottlenecks Customers face frustratingly slow queue lines during lunch hours, consuming up to half of their allocated break time before ordering.
2. Verbal Miscommunication & Mistakes Loud cafeteria backdrops frequently result in cashiers misinterpreting custom toppings and ingredient exclusions, causing wrong orders.
3. Frictional Payment & Checkout A lack of self-serve payment channels forces customers to stand in secondary lines purely to pay or handle physical cash.
4. Opaque Status Tracking Once ordered, customers are left in the dark about when their food is being prepared versus when it is ready for collection at the counter.

04 Solution

A streamlined touchscreen solution designed to minimize cognitive load, accelerate checkout speed, and empower users with total customization control.

The interface implements an optimized, low-friction user journey designed around core HCI usability heuristics. Specifically, we leveraged recognition over recall (using prominent visual food photography/imagery instead of plain text lists) and maximized user control and freedom (providing a permanently visible sidebar cart for instant order modification).

05 Wireframes

Validating early layout structures through quick user feedback loops.
Low-Fidelity Concept
Simplifying Navigation Flow

Early paper wireframes mapped a unified left sidebar for category selection to avoid multi-level drop-down menu navigation. Visual elements were placed in clear grid boxes for straightforward scanning.

Initial Testing Feedback
Category Grouping Problems

Initial user testing of low-fidelity wires indicated that users required larger touch targets for accessibility. Grouping categories by large icons rather than just text became a key refinement.

06 High-Fidelity UI

Polishing interactive states and visual cues for intuitive touchscreen use.
Visual Identity
Consistent Category Cards

Implemented category lists featuring distinct food emojis and bold typography headers. Buttons use high-contrast primary styles to establish clear calls-to-action.

Interaction Polish
Seamless Customization Drawer

Designed a simple, pop-up customization modal overlay. Options for exclusions and add-ons are structured as large, finger-friendly checkboxes to accelerate selection speed.

Before & After Design Evolution

Challenge 1: Menu Navigation & Categories

Touch Targets & Recall
Before (Low-Fi Wireframe)
  • Plain text category navigation list without any icon recognition support.
  • Narrow button boundaries (under 30px touch height) causing frequent mis-taps.
  • No active indicator styling to clearly highlight selected sections.
After (High-Fi UI Refinements)
  • Visual Emojis: Accelerates category scanning using visual recognition over text recall.
  • Tactile Sizing: Height expanded to 48px to accommodate target ergonomics.
  • Active State Glow: Linear active gradients emphasize active selection indicators.

Challenge 2: Meal Customization & Add-ons Flow

Customization Efficiency
Before (Low-Fi Wireframe)
  • Exclusions and add-ons spread across separate popups, raising transition steps.
  • Narrow checkbox input points that are difficult to trigger at arm's length.
  • Pricing modifications are not calculated visually prior to drawer closure.
After (High-Fi UI Refinements)
  • Unified Drawer Modal: Consolidates custom options into a single layout.
  • Expanded Check Areas: Configured larger touch boundary boxes.
  • Real-time Pricing: Added automatic price visual calculations to items.

Accelerated Checkout & Customization By leveraging progressive disclosure on meal customizations and permanently displaying a sidebar cart, users completed their order configurations without second-guessing, saving an average of 90 seconds per transaction.
Visual Validation Mitigates Errors Large visual food representations and explicit item reviews before checkout completely resolved customization mishaps. In noisy restaurant environments, this visual-first validation replaced error-prone verbal cashier interactions.

09 Reflection

Designing for extremes yields a more accessible, successful product for all users.
  • Dichotomy works: Designing to accommodate low-tech confidence Shanthi naturally created a cleaner, faster, and more efficient experience for high-urgency Kavindu.
  • Explicit confirmation loops: Fast, noisy cafeteria environments require highly explicit checkout confirmations to prevent cart abandonment and user confusion.
  • Evidence over assumptions: Direct field observations corrected early layout bottlenecks before writing code, proving that UX evaluation loops are critical.
